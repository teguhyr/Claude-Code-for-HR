## 1. Dave Ulrich's HR Model — Deep Dive

### Background

Dave Ulrich introduced his HR model in *Human Resource Champions* (1997). It defined four distinct roles HR must play to add value to an organization. It remains one of the most widely used frameworks in strategic HR.

The core insight: HR cannot just be an administrative function. To drive business outcomes, HR must operate simultaneously as a strategic thinker, an operational expert, an employee advocate, and a change leader.

### The Four-Role Model

| Role | Focus | What HR does | Claude Code support |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategic Partner** | Business outcomes | Aligns HR strategy to company goals; sits at the leadership table | Draft HR strategy docs, business review reports, workforce planning narratives |
| **Administrative Expert** | Efficiency and accuracy | Designs and runs compliant, consistent HR processes | Generate templates, SOPs, policy docs, compliance checklists |
| **Employee Champion** | People advocacy | Listens to and advocates for employees; builds engagement | Engagement survey analysis, recognition program content, employee comms |
| **Change Agent** | Transformation | Leads and supports organizational change | Change communication plans, stakeholder maps, FAQ documents |

### The HRBP Evolution

After Ulrich, organizations began structuring HR around **HR Business Partners (HRBPs)** — senior HR professionals embedded within business units who operate across all four roles simultaneously.

An HRBP in practice:
- Attends business unit leadership meetings (Strategic Partner)
- Ensures processes run smoothly for their business unit (Administrative Expert)
- Advocates for employees in that unit (Employee Champion)
- Leads change initiatives within the unit (Change Agent)

Claude Code helps HRBPs produce the written deliverables each role demands — faster and more consistently.

## 2. Josh Bersin's Employee Experience — Deep Dive

### Background

Josh Bersin, founder of Bersin by Deloitte and author of *Irresistible* (2022), argues that the future of HR is designing a holistic **Employee Experience (EX)** — not just running programs, but shaping how employees feel every day at work.

**EX definition:** Every interaction an employee has with the organization — from seeing the job ad to becoming an alumni — is part of their experience. HR's job is to make that experience consistently positive, meaningful, and productive.

### The Three Pillars of Employee Experience

| Pillar | What it means | HR focus areas |
|---|---|---|
| **Productivity** | Do employees have what they need to do great work? | Tools, processes, workload, clarity of role, quality of manager |
| **Wellbeing** | Are employees physically, mentally, and financially healthy? | Mental health support, flexible work, financial wellness, caregiver support |
| **Meaning** | Do employees find purpose, growth, and belonging at work? | Mission alignment, career development, recognition, diversity and inclusion |

### Moments that Matter

Bersin's research identifies specific "moments" in an employee's journey that disproportionately shape their overall experience. These are high-emotion, high-stakes interactions — and HR must design them intentionally.

Examples of moments that matter:
- First day at work
- First performance review
- First time managing someone
- Returning from parental leave
- Being passed over for a promotion
- Deciding whether to stay or leave

### Exercises

**Exercise 3.4 — EX Journey Map**

```bash

- Name the moment (e.g., 'First Day', 'First Performance Review')
- Describe what typically happens
- Rate the current experience: Positive / Neutral / Negative
- Identify what HR can do to improve this moment
- Suggest one Claude Code prompt HR could use to support this moment
Format as a table." > output/ex-journey-map-[persona].md
```

```bash
- Key questions to ask (5 per stage)
- Who owns the data
```

## 3. Combining Ulrich + Bersin: Strategic HR in Practice

### The Integration Matrix

Use this matrix to decide where Claude Code creates the most strategic leverage for your HR team.

| | **Productivity (Bersin)** | **Wellbeing (Bersin)** | **Meaning (Bersin)** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategic Partner (Ulrich)** | Workforce planning aligned to output goals | Mental health as a business performance driver | Purpose-aligned HR strategy |
| **Administrative Expert (Ulrich)** | Streamlined tools and process design | Automated wellbeing check-ins and admin | Recognition and career path documentation |
| **Employee Champion (Ulrich)** | Listening to workload and friction points | Advocacy for flexible and healthy work | Belonging programs and growth conversations |
| **Change Agent (Ulrich)** | Change readiness and adoption support | Supporting people through difficult transitions | Building culture during change |

**How to use this matrix:** Pick one cell per quarter as your HR team's strategic focus. Use Claude Code to produce the written deliverables for that focus area.

### Exercise 3.9 — HR Annual Plan (Capstone)

```bash
claude -p "Write a one-page HR Annual Plan for [COMPANY NAME] for [YEAR].
Structure it using:
- Dave Ulrich's four HR roles as the organizational framework
- Josh Bersin's three EX pillars (Productivity, Wellbeing, Meaning) as the employee-facing priorities
For each Ulrich role, list 2-3 initiatives. For each initiative, note which Bersin EX pillar it serves.
Include: success metrics, key dependencies, and one risk per initiative.
Keep it to one page — executive summary format." > output/hr-annual-plan-[year].md
```

## 5. Change Management Communications

Change management is one of the highest-value areas for HR writing. The ADKAR model (Prosci) gives you a five-stage structure: **Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement**.

**Exercise 3.12 — ADKAR Communication Campaign**

Run five prompts in sequence — one email per ADKAR stage — for a specific organizational change:

```bash
# Stage 1: Awareness
claude -p "Write an email to all employees announcing [CHANGE]. Focus on WHAT is changing and WHY. Be transparent about the reason. 200 words max." > output/change-adkar-1-awareness.md

# Stage 2: Desire
claude -p "Write a follow-up message that helps employees understand WIIFM (What's In It For Me) about [CHANGE]. Focus on benefits to individuals and teams. 200 words max." > output/change-adkar-2-desire.md

# Stage 3: Knowledge
claude -p "Write a communication explaining HOW [CHANGE] will work in practice. Include a FAQ with 5 questions and clear answers. 300 words max." > output/change-adkar-3-knowledge.md

# Stage 4: Ability
claude -p "Write a message telling employees what support is available to help them adapt to [CHANGE]. Include training, resources, who to contact. 200 words max." > output/change-adkar-4-ability.md

# Stage 5: Reinforcement
claude -p "Write a 30-day follow-up message celebrating early wins from [CHANGE] and reinforcing the new way of working. Acknowledge it has been an adjustment. 200 words max." > output/change-adkar-5-reinforcement.md
```


### Versioning HR documents with git (plain English)

Git is a tool that tracks changes to text files over time. For HR, this means:
- You can see who changed a policy and when
- You can roll back to an older version of a document
- You can share a document with a colleague and merge their edits

Basic git commands for HR document management:

```bash
git add output/remote-work-policy.md     # Stage a file for saving
git commit -m "Update remote work policy for hybrid rollout"  # Save with a label
git log                                  # See history of all changes
git diff                                 # See what changed since last save
```

You do not need to be a developer to use git for HR documents. Think of it as a tracked version history — like "Track Changes" in Word, but more powerful.
